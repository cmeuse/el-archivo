# EL ARCHIVO
## Song Annotations: Music as Decolonial Poetry

---

### Resistance & Form

**1. "Safaera" — *YHLQMDLG* (2020)**

Five-minute genre collage that feels like a nine-minute barrage, sampling multiple tracks including DJ Playero and Missy Elliott, threading through dembow, perreo, electronic, and reggae [1]. Enacts what scholars call the 'decolonial cimarronaje' of reggaetón, constant genre escape that refuses colonial classification [2]. DJ scratches and mid-song transitions mirror Puerto Rican code-switching; the refusal to settle into one register is itself political.

---

**2. "Baile Inolvidable" — *Debí Tirar Más Fotos* (2025)**

Six-minute salsa track featuring orchestral elements. Operates on a double register: ostensibly about heartbreak, but reread with Puerto Rico as subject it becomes a meditation on exile and the impossibility of forgetting home [3]. Salsa itself carries political weight — it emerged from the Nuyorican diaspora in the 1960s–70s as a sound of Puerto Rican survival in New York. Returning to this form in 2025 is an act of musical repatriation [4].

---

**3. "MIA" ft. Drake — *X 100PRE* (2018)**

Drake, an English-language superstar, sings entirely in Spanish alongside Bad Bunny, staging Spanish as sufficient for global pop without translation [5]. Code-switching occurs at the seam between the two artists' verses, with both performing in Spanish — a structural choice placing Spanish in parallel with Drake's typical English-language dominance. Part of the broader *X 100PRE* project of introducing a fully Spanish-language global pop star to English-language markets, a deliberate refusal to translate [6].

---

**4. "Callaíta" — Single (2019)**

Released May 2019, two months before the Rosselló protests [7]. The *perreo combativo* (combative twerking) that protestors deployed outside La Fortaleza made bodily pleasure an explicit instrument of resistance, retroactively loading this song with political weight [8]. Scholars of Caribbean decolonial feminism have noted that perreo, long stigmatized as obscene, became during the 2019 protests a collective act of civic will [9]. The 'silent' woman who acts is a recurring figure in Caribbean feminist practice.

---

### Diaspora & Migration

**5. "NUEVAYoL" — *Debí Tirar Más Fotos* (2025)**

Opening track of DTMF, addressing hundreds of thousands of Puerto Ricans in New York City [10]. Samples salsa references layered over Dominican dembow rhythms, a gesture of pan-Caribbean solidarity [11]. Music video released July 4, 2025, shows Bad Bunny dancing inside the Statue of Liberty with a Puerto Rican flag over the crown — American independence as backdrop to colonial subject's refusal of assimilation [12][13]. Connects to Nuyorican literary tradition: Piri Thomas, Pedro Pietri, Miguel Piñero.

---

**6. "Voy a LLeVARte Pa PR" — *Debí Tirar Más Fotos* (2025)**

Track two of DTMF [14]. Read alongside "NUEVAYoL" as a deliberate diptych: one faces the diaspora (New York), the other faces the island (Puerto Rico), mapping the full emotional circuit of displacement and return. Inverts the typical directionality of diaspora narrative — Puerto Rico as destination, not departure point.

---

**7. "Estamos Bien" — *X 100PRE* (2018)**

Released Christmas Eve 2018, one year after Hurricane Maria [15]. The phrase 'estamos bien' was used satirically and seriously by Puerto Ricans post-Maria, a collective coping mechanism, a refusal to be seen as victim. Government's official death toll stood at 64 for nearly a year before independent researchers established 2,975 [16][17]. Bad Bunny's Tonight Show performance ("more than 3,000 people died, and Trump is still in denial") was one of the most high-profile rebuttals of that denial [18][19].

---

### Gentrification & Place

**8. "El Apagón" — *Un Verano Sin Ti* (2022)**

Uses the island's chronic power outages, worsened after LUMA Energy privatization (June 2021), as metaphor for colonial extraction [20]. Accompanied by 22-minute documentary *Aquí Vive Gente*, featuring investigative journalist Bianca Graulau [21][22].

> **Key policy context:** Act 20 (2012) and Act 22 (2012), collectively Act 60 after 2019, offer near-zero capital gains taxes for Americans establishing Puerto Rico residency for at least half the year [23]. Result: documented wave of wealthy mainland Americans purchasing coastal properties, inflating real estate, displacing Puerto Rican families. LUMA Energy: Canadian-American consortium took over Puerto Rico's power grid June 2021; blackouts increased, rates rose [20][24]. Bad Bunny called out Governor Pierluisi and LUMA from the stage at San Juan Choliseo, July 2022 [25].

---

**9. "Lo Que Le Pasó a Hawaii" — *Debí Tirar Más Fotos* (2025)**

Extends gentrification argument into comparative colonial framework [26]. Hawaii became the 50th U.S. state in 1959 after decades of American colonial administration, displacement of native Hawaiians, militarization, conversion of agricultural economy to mass tourism, language extinction, land alienation, cultural erasure. Bad Bunny's lyric frames statehood debate as a life-or-death question for Puerto Rican cultural survival. Accompanying short film shows filmmaker Jacobo Morales wandering a gentrified neighborhood unrecognizable to him [27].

---

**10. "La Mudanza" — *Debí Tirar Más Fotos* (2025)**

Closing track of DTMF [28]. References the 1948 Gag Law (Law 53 of 1948), passed under U.S. pressure, which made it a felony to display a Puerto Rican flag, sing a patriotic tune, or speak against the U.S. government in Puerto Rico [29]. Enforced against the Puerto Rican Nationalist Party; on the books until 1957. The right to stay on the island as the successor political claim to the right to carry the flag.

---

### State Violence, Corruption & Civic Resistance

**11. "Afilando los Cuchillos" ft. Residente & iLe (2019)**

Released July 17, 2019, four days after Telegramgate broke [30][31]. Released directly to YouTube, circulated through WhatsApp, became a protest anthem within hours. Bad Bunny cut short his European tour to return to Puerto Rico and march [32].

> **Telegramgate context:** On July 13, 2019, the CPI (Centro de Periodismo Investigativo) published approximately 900 pages of a private Telegram chat between Governor Rosselló and 11 aides — jokes mocking Maria's 2,975 dead, homophobic slurs, misogynistic attacks, references to corruption [33][34]. Largest protests in Puerto Rican history followed. Estimated 500,000 marched July 22. Rosselló resigned July 24 [35]. The diss-track form, borrowed from hip-hop, was deliberately chosen for its capacity to personalize and humiliate.

---

**12. "Yo Perreo Sola" — *YHLQMDLG* (2020)**

Music video featured Bad Bunny in drag, performing in heels [36]. Released in the context of Puerto Rico's femicide crisis and the murder of trans woman Alexa Neulisa Luciano Ruiz (shot in Toa Baja, February 23, 2020) [37][38]. Bad Bunny's Tonight Show appearance, March 4, 2020, wore shirt reading 'MATARON A ALEXA, NO A UN HOMBRE CON FALDA' ('They killed Alexa, not a man in a skirt'), broadcast live to millions [39][40]. Puerto Rico's femicide rate declared a state of emergency in 2021 [41]. Part of the political wave that included the 2019 protests.

---

### Memory & Decolonial Futures

**13. "Debí Tirar Más Fotos" (title track) — *Debí Tirar Más Fotos* (2025)**

Title track meditates on regret for undocumented moments — ostensibly romantic, but in context becomes a meditation on failing to document a Puerto Rico being erased: neighborhoods gentrified, communities displaced, cultural life commodified [42]. YouTube visualizers for all 17 DTMF tracks, produced with historian Jorell Meléndez-Badillo, compile Puerto Rican history: Spanish colonization, U.S. acquisition after 1898, diaspora, repression, killing of student activist Antonia Martínez, the independence flag [43][44]. Turns the album's digital release into an educational archive.

---

**14. "El Mundo Es Mío" — Single (2016)**

Bad Bunny's early viral track came while working as a supermarket bagger in Vega Baja, self-recorded and uploaded to SoundCloud [45][46]. Origin story of a decolonial subject who insists on his own validity before anyone else agrees. The form of release — unauthorized, outside industry structures — mirrors the bootleg, DIY circulation of early reggaetón on mix CDs and pirated cassettes. Archival starting point: the 'before' against which the full political project is measured.

## Key Song Connections

Song nodes connected by shared arguments, formal strategies, or historical references:

- **El Apagón ↔ Lo Que Le Pasó a Hawaii:** Both identify a U.S. colonial policy as the mechanism of displacement (Act 60; statehood); both use a spatial metaphor as the consequence (darkened island; Hawaiianized island).

- **Afilando los Cuchillos ↔ Estamos Bien:** Hurricane Maria arc: 'Estamos Bien' documents survival and governmental neglect (2018); 'Afilando' documents the explosion when Rosselló's chats revealed him mocking the Maria dead (2019).

- **NUEVAYoL ↔ Voy a LLeVARte Pa PR:** Deliberate diptych opening DTMF; one faces the diaspora, one faces the island. Together map the emotional circuit of displacement.

- **La Mudanza ↔ Afilando los Cuchillos:** Both assert territorial claim using the same logic of popular sovereignty: one forces a corrupt official out, one refuses displacement by gentrifiers.

- **Safaera ↔ Baile Inolvidable:** Both use music-historical reference as the primary political gesture; together say: we remember where this music came from.

- **Yo Perreo Sola ↔ Callaíta:** Both circulated during the 2019 political cycle; together trace the political trajectory of the female dancing body from background to foreground.

- **Debí Tirar Más Fotos ↔ El Mundo Es Mío:** Temporal arc of the full project: the assertion before the world agrees (2016) → the retrospective grief at not having documented the world you were insisting was yours (2025).

- **MIA ↔ NUEVAYoL:** Both engage the politics of Spanish in Anglophone space; 'MIA' stages structural contrast with Drake performing in Spanish; 'NUEVAYoL' blends Spanish with New York cultural context that is neither fully Spanish nor fully English.

## Political Timeline: Key Events & Releases

### 2016

**PROMESA signed (June 30, 2016):** Creates unelected fiscal oversight board (La Junta) with authority over Puerto Rico's budget, pensions, public institutions [57]. Puerto Rico is $72 billion in public debt. Critics: an extension of colonial control, removing democratic authority from elected officials.

**Early viral track (SoundCloud, 2016):** Self-recorded, uploaded while working as a supermarket bagger in Vega Baja [58][59]. Year Puerto Rican democratic authority was most explicitly overridden is the year the artist who would become its most powerful cultural voice announced himself.

---

### 2017

**Hurricane Maria (September 20, 2017):** Category 4, strongest since 1928. Destroyed the island's entire power grid. 2,975 dead (government reported 64 for nearly a year) [60][61]. $91.6 billion in damage. 135,000+ Puerto Ricans emigrated to the continental U.S. in 18 months. Trump's response widely criticized — threw paper towels into a crowd, disputed death toll, complained Puerto Rico was disrupting the federal budget [62]. FEMA contract awarded to a two-person Montana company with Trump cabinet ties.

No Bad Bunny release in 2017. Participated in Somos Live! hurricane relief concert; wore shirt '¿Eres Twitero o Presidente?' criticizing Trump's Twitter activity during the emergency.

---

### 2018

**Death toll cover-up exposed:** Harvard T.H. Chan School of Public Health estimates true toll at 4,645 (August 2018) [63]. Puerto Rico government commissions study arriving at 2,975 (September 2018). Governor Rosselló accepts new figure after nearly a year of denial [64].

***X 100PRE* (Christmas Eve 2018), including "Estamos Bien":** Tonight Show performance opens with direct statement about ongoing displacement, deaths, and Trump's denial [65][66]. Bad Bunny's political voice breaks into American mainstream consciousness.

---

### 2019

**Kevin Fret murdered (January 10, 2019):** Puerto Rico's first openly gay trap artist shot in Santurce. Bad Bunny and Residente stand outside La Fortaleza same day demanding accountability [67]. Catalyzes connection between LGBTQ+ rights and broader civic movement.

**Telegramgate / Rosselló resignation (July 2019):** CPI publishes approximately 900 pages of Telegram chats between Governor Rosselló and 11 aides — jokes about Maria dead, homophobic slurs, misogynistic attacks, corruption references [68][69]. Largest protests in Puerto Rican history. 500,000 march July 22. Rosselló resigns July 24 [70].

**"Afilando los Cuchillos" ft. Residente & iLe (July 17, 2019):** Released 4 days after Telegramgate broke [71][72]. Released to YouTube, circulated by WhatsApp, playing at protests within hours. Bad Bunny flies back from European tour. Rosselló resigns 7 days after release — most direct relationship between a Bad Bunny release and a political event in the timeline.

**"Callaíta" (May 2019):** Two months before protests; becomes part of protest soundtrack retroactively via *perreo combativo* outside La Fortaleza [73].

---

### 2020

**Earthquakes (January 6–7, 2020):** 6.4 magnitude centered near Guánica [74]. Collapses power grid, third major failure in three years. FEMA slow to distribute aid.

**Murder of Alexa Neulisa Luciano Ruiz (February 23, 2020):** Trans woman shot in Toa Baja. Police and media repeatedly misgender her [75][76]. Bad Bunny wears 'MATARON A ALEXA, NO A UN HOMBRE CON FALDA' shirt on Tonight Show March 4, 2020, broadcast live to millions [77][78].

***YHLQMDLG* (February 29, 2020), including "Yo Perreo Sola":** Spotify's most-streamed album globally 2020 [79]. Released 2 days after the Alexa Tonight Show appearance. Album title ('I Do What I Want') reads as an assertion of agency while the Puerto Rican government was again failing its people.

---

### 2021

**LUMA Energy takes over power grid (June 1, 2021):** 15-year contract, $2.4 billion, to Canadian-American consortium [80]. Blackouts increase, rates rise. Becomes central target of 'El Apagón' the following year.

**Femicide state of emergency declared (January 2021):** Puerto Rico has one of the highest femicide rates in the U.S. [81]

---

### 2022

**Former Governor Wanda Vázquez arrested on federal bribery charges (August 2022):** First former Puerto Rican governor to face federal criminal charges [82].

**Hurricane Fiona (September 2022):** Record rainfall, island-wide blackout, fourth major grid failure since Maria [83]. $3.2 billion in damage. LUMA Energy's failure widely blamed.

***Un Verano Sin Ti* (May 6, 2022), including "El Apagón":** Most-streamed album globally 2022 (IFPI) [84]. 'El Apagón' directly targets LUMA and Pierluisi. First Spanish-language album nominated for Grammy Album of the Year [85].

---

### 2024–2025

**2024 gubernatorial election:** Bad Bunny publicly endorses Juan Dalmau, Puerto Rican Independence Party — first prominent celebrity endorsement of an independence candidate in decades [86].

***Debí Tirar Más Fotos* (January 5, 2025):** Released 2 weeks before Trump's second inauguration [87]. Blends plena, salsa, bomba, jíbaro, dembow. YouTube visualizers with historian Jorell Meléndez-Badillo provide Puerto Rican history alongside each track [88][89]. Won Album of the Year at the 2026 Grammy Awards, first Spanish-language album in history to win the top prize [90][91][92].

**Super Bowl LX Halftime Show (February 9, 2025, New Orleans):** Accepted invitation as protest. Performed 'El Apagón' in full, LUMA Energy lyrics and displacement messaging broadcast to 100+ million viewers [93].

**ICE raids in Puerto Rico (June 2025):** Bad Bunny posts video criticizing ICE targeting American citizens in a U.S. territory, exposes colonial logic treating Puerto Ricans as perpetual foreigners regardless of legal status [94][95].
