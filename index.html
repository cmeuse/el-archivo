01
"Safaera"
YHLQMDLG· 2020·9 min.
A: Decolonial Poetry
▾
"Safaera, safaera, safaera / Tú no sabes ná' de mí, cabrona"
"You know nothing about me — an assertion of opacity against any expectation of legibility."
— Safaera (2020)
Annotation Notes

A nine-minute genre collage that samples across at least five distinct musical worlds — most prominently Missy Elliott's "Get Ur Freak On," Bob Marley's "Could You Be Loved," and Shaggy & Rayvon's "Big Up," layered over classic dembow and perreo. The song enacts the cimarronaje of reggaetón: a fugitive practice of constant genre escape that refuses colonial classification. Each DJ scratch mirrors Puerto Rican code-switching — the refusal to settle into one register is itself political.

Cross-reference

Bad Bunny Syllabus microsyllabus on reggaetón history; Guimond on genre politics as decolonial practice.

02
"Baile Inolvidable"
Debí Tirar Más Fotos·2025·6 min.
A: Decolonial PoetryE: Memory
▾
"Gracias por enseñarme a bailar / Y a querer, que esto no se borra"
"Thank you for teaching me to dance / And to love — this cannot be erased."
— Baile Inolvidable (2025)
Annotation Notes

Six-minute salsa track featuring students from the Escuela Libre de Música in San Juan. Lyrics operate on a double register: ostensibly about heartbreak, but reread with Puerto Rico as the subject, it becomes a meditation on exile and the impossibility of forgetting home. Styled after Héctor Lavoe — a Nuyorican salsa singer — the form itself carries political weight. Salsa emerged from the 1960s–70s Nuyorican community as the sound of Puerto Rican survival in New York.

Cross-reference

Rodriguez-Madera et al. on Bad Bunny as cultural archive; Barreiro León on form as political choice; Nuyorican genealogy: Héctor Lavoe → El Gran Combo → DTMF.

03
"MIA" ft. Drake
X 100PRE·2018
A: Decolonial PoetryB: Diaspora
▾
"Tú eres mía, mía, mía / Tú eres mía, para siempre mía"
"You are mine — possessive language that enacts linguistic ownership: this is my language, my register."
— MIA (2018)
Annotation Notes

The collaboration with Drake stages Spanish and English in structural contrast — two parallel worlds that do not collapse into each other. Bad Bunny raps exclusively in Spanish against Drake's English verse. The Spanglish title "MIA" = Miami (U.S.) but also the Spanish "mía" (mine). This was the album that introduced a fully Spanish-language global star to English-language markets — a deliberate refusal to translate for mainstream accessibility.

Cross-reference

Barreiro León on code-switching and Spanglish politics; the broader X 100PRE project as language-sovereignty statement.

04
"Callaíta"
Single·May 2019
A: Decolonial PoetryD: Civic Resistance
▾
"Callaíta, tú eres mala / Pero me gustas así"
"Quiet one, you are bad / But I like you that way — the silent woman who acts: refusal to perform legibility."
— Callaíta (2019)
Annotation Notes

Released May 2019, two months before the Rosselló protests. The perreo combativo — women dancing perreo outside La Fortaleza as political protest — retroactively made "Callaíta" part of the protest soundtrack. A governor whose leaked chats showed contempt for women was confronted with women exercising bodily autonomy in public. Caribbean decolonial feminist scholars argue that joy in the face of colonial repression is itself resistance.

Cross-reference

Rodriguez-Madera et al. on perreo as political act; Guimond on the decolonial body.

05
"NUEVAYoL"
Debí Tirar Más Fotos·2025
B: Diaspora & Migration
▾
"NUEVAYoL, la gente te lleva en el corazón / Pero sigue soñando con volver al rincón"
"New York, people carry you in their hearts / But they keep dreaming of returning to the corner — 'el rincón' is the irreducible unit of Puerto Rican place-memory."
— NUEVAYoL (2025)
Annotation Notes

Opens DTMF by addressing the approximately one million Puerto Ricans in New York. Samples El Gran Combo de Puerto Rico's "Un Verano en Nueva York" and layers it over Dominican dembow — a gesture of pan-Caribbean solidarity. Music video released July 4, 2025: Bad Bunny dances inside the Statue of Liberty with a Puerto Rican flag draped over the crown, transforming Independence Day into a confrontation with colonial reality.

Cross-reference

Barreiro León on Nuyorican identity politics; Nuyorican literary genealogy: Piri Thomas, Pedro Pietri, Miguel Piñero, Nuyorican Poets Cafe.

06
"Voy a LLeVARte Pa PR"
Debí Tirar Más Fotos·2025
B: Diaspora & Migration
▾
"Te voy a llevar pa' Puerto Rico / Pa' que tú conozcas lo que es bueno"
"I'm going to take you to Puerto Rico / So you can know what's good — a reversal of the migration script."
— Voy a LLeVARte Pa PR (2025)
Annotation Notes

Track two of DTMF immediately repositions the album after "NUEVAYoL." Together these two songs form a deliberate diptych: the album honors the diaspora first, then calls them home. Puerto Rico as destination rather than departure inverts the typical directionality of diaspora narrative — after honoring the life built abroad, DTMF argues the island is worth returning to. Annotate alongside "NUEVAYoL" as an inseparable pair.

07
"Estamos Bien"
X 100PRE·2018
B: DiasporaD: Resistance
▾
"Estamos bien, pa' los que preguntan / Estamos bien, aunque nos faltan cosas"
"We are fine, for those who ask / We are fine, even though we are missing things — defiant affirmation that contains the full weight of what has been lost."
— Estamos Bien (2018)
Annotation Notes

Released in the year after Hurricane Maria. "Estamos bien" became a collective coping mechanism — an act of refusal to be seen as victim. The government's death toll stood at 64 for nearly a year before researchers established it at 2,975. Bad Bunny's Tonight Show performance stated: "After one year of the hurricane, there's still people without electricity — more than 3,000 people died, and Trump is still in denial." His political voice breaking into American consciousness for the first time.

Cross-reference

Rodriguez-Madera et al. on disaster and cultural response; Harvard NEJM study (2018) on Maria death toll.

08
"El Apagón"
Un Verano Sin Ti·2022·w/ documentary: Aquí Vive Gente
C: GentrificationD: Resistance
▾
"Si no quieres ver a Puerto Rico florecer / Vete pa' la puta, yo me quedo aquí"
"If you don't want to see Puerto Rico flourish / Get the fuck out — I'm staying here."
— El Apagón (2022)
"No me quiero ir de aquí, que se vayan ellos / Lo que es mío lo cogen prestado"
"I don't want to leave here, let them go / What is mine they take on loan." — Sung by Gabriela Berlingeri in the outro.
— El Apagón outro (2022)
Annotation Notes

LUMA Energy: In June 2021, Puerto Rico handed over power transmission to LUMA Energy, a private Canadian-American consortium. Blackouts increased; rates rose. Bad Bunny called out LUMA and Governor Pierluisi directly at Choliseo in July 2022. Act 60 (formerly Acts 20/22): Tax incentives offering near-zero capital gains taxes to Americans who establish Puerto Rico residency — driving displacement in Santurce, Rincón, and Puerta de Tierra. The 22-minute documentary Aquí Vive Gente features investigative journalist Bianca Graulau and documents these policies directly.

Academic Source

IASPM Journal: "Cultural Identity, Colonialism, and Gentrification in Puerto Rico: Bad Bunny's El Apagón as a Case Study."

09
"Lo Que Le Pasó a Hawaii"
Debí Tirar Más Fotos·2025
C: Gentrification
▾
"Que no nos pase lo que le pasó a Hawaii / Que nuestra gente salga a votar aquí"
"May what happened to Hawaii not happen to us / May our people come out to vote here — an explicitly anti-statehood statement invoking Hawaii as cautionary precedent."
— Lo Que Le Pasó a Hawaii (2025)
Annotation Notes

Draws a direct line between Puerto Rico's displacement crisis and Hawaii's experience of Americanization: suppression of indigenous language, privatization of land, conversion to a tourist economy. Hawaii became the 50th state in 1959 after U.S. colonial administration that displaced Native Hawaiians. Bad Bunny's lyric frames statehood as a life-or-death question for Puerto Rican cultural survival. The accompanying short film shows filmmaker Jacobo Morales wandering a gentrified neighborhood he no longer recognizes.

10
"La Mudanza"
Debí Tirar Más Fotos·2025
C: GentrificationE: Memory
▾
"De aquí nadie me saca, de aquí yo no me muevo / Dile que esta es mi casa donde nació mi abuelo"
"No one will kick me out of here / Tell them this is my home where my grandfather was born — genealogy as title deed."
— La Mudanza (2025)
"Murieron por cargar la bandera nuestra / Los mataron por gritar que somos libres"
"They died for carrying our flag / They were killed for shouting that we are free — reference to the 1948 Gag Law."
— La Mudanza (2025)
Annotation Notes

Law 53 of 1948 (the "Gag Law"): Made it a felony to display a Puerto Rican flag, sing a patriotic tune, or write against the U.S. government. Enforced against the Nationalist Party; remained on the books until 1957. "La Mudanza" connects this history directly to contemporary anti-displacement politics — the right to stay on the island as the successor political claim to the right to carry the flag. The closing track of DTMF ends the album as a declaration of refusal.

11
"Afilando los Cuchillos" ft. Residente & iLe
Single·July 17, 2019
D: State Violence & Resistance
▾
"Estamos afilando los cuchillos / Pa' sacar la hierba mala del bohío"
"We are sharpening the knives / To pull the weeds from the homestead — 'bohío' (Taíno dwelling) grounds resistance in Indigenous history."
— Afilando los Cuchillos (2019)
Annotation Notes

"Telegramgate" / RickyLeaks: On July 13, 2019, the Centro de Periodismo Investigativo published ~900 pages of chats between Governor Rosselló and 11 aides — jokes mocking the 2,975 Maria dead, homophobic slurs, misogynistic attacks, corruption references. Released four days after the scandal broke, this song became the protest anthem within hours. Bad Bunny flew back from his European tour. An estimated 500,000 people marched on July 22. Rosselló resigned on July 24 — seven days after the song dropped. The diss-track form is itself a political choice: it personalizes and humiliates with precision.

12
"Yo Perreo Sola"
YHLQMDLG·2020
D: ResistanceA: Decolonial Poetry
▾
"Ella baila sola / Y a ella le gusta, no necesita a nadie"
"She dances alone / And she likes it — the right to occupy public space on one's own terms."
— Yo Perreo Sola (2020)
Annotation Notes

Music video featured Bad Bunny in full drag — prosthetic female body, heels — performing the song. Released in the context of Puerto Rico's femicide crisis. Alexa Neulisa Luciano Ruiz was murdered in Toa Baja on February 24, 2020. Police and media repeatedly misgendered her. Bad Bunny's Tonight Show appearance March 4, 2020 — wearing a shirt reading "MATARON A ALEXA, NO A UN HOMBRE CON FALDA" — broadcast a trans rights statement to millions of American viewers. Puerto Rico declared femicide a state of emergency in January 2021.

Cross-reference

Duquesne University dissertation: "Analyzing Decolonial Subjectivities in Reggaeton and Latin-Trap Music" — analyzes "Yo Perreo Sola" as a feminist decolonial text.

13
"Debí Tirar Más Fotos" (title track)
Debí Tirar Más Fotos·2025
E: Memory & Decolonial Futures
▾
"Debí tirar más fotos / Guardar más momentos"
"I should have taken more photos / Saved more moments — the archive as decolonial practice; documentation against erasure."
— Debí Tirar Más Fotos (2025)
Annotation Notes

Ostensibly about not documenting a love story enough; in context, it is a meditation on failing to document a Puerto Rico being erased: neighborhoods gentrified, communities displaced, cultural life commodified. The YouTube visualizers for all 17 DTMF tracks, produced with historian Jorell Meléndez-Badillo, compile Puerto Rican history alongside each song — turning the album's digital release into an educational archive. Note: EL ARCHIVO itself is an enactment of the album's thesis — this project is taking more photos.

14
"El Mundo Es Mío"
Single (SoundCloud)·2016
E: Memory & Decolonial Futures
▾
"El mundo es mío y todavía no lo saben"
"The world is mine and they don't know it yet — the most compressed statement of the decolonial subject's position."
— El Mundo Es Mío (2016)
Annotation Notes

Self-produced and uploaded to SoundCloud while Bad Bunny was still working as a supermarket bagger in Bayamón. The form of release mirrors the bootleg, DIY circulation of early reggaetón on mix CDs — taking the means of distribution. As the earliest track in the EL ARCHIVO corpus, it establishes the "before" against which the whole political arc can be measured. Read alongside "Debí Tirar Más Fotos" (2025) as a temporal diptych: the assertion before the world agrees (2016) and the retrospective grief at not having documented the world you were insisting was yours (2025).

02
Constellation View
Songs as stars, frameworks as nebulae — thematic edges as light between worlds
The Constellation View renders the 14 songs as nodes in a force-directed star map. Each song gravitates toward its primary framework hub. Connections represent shared arguments — the political case emerges from the density of edges, not any single song. Hover to illuminate connections.

Key Edge Relationships

El Apagón ↔ Lo Que Le Pasó a Hawaii

Shared argument: colonial extraction as spatial practice. Both identify a U.S. policy as the mechanism of displacement — the darkened island, the Hawaiianized island. Edge label: colonial dispossession.

Afilando los Cuchillos ↔ Estamos Bien

Shared reference: Hurricane Maria's political aftermath. "Estamos Bien" documents survival and neglect. "Afilando" documents the explosion when chats revealed Rosselló mocking the Maria dead. Edge label: Maria → accountability.

NUEVAYoL ↔ Voy a LLeVARte Pa PR

Structural relationship: deliberate diptych opening DTMF. One faces the diaspora (New York); the other faces the island. Edge label: diaspora circuit.

Yo Perreo Sola ↔ Callaíta

"Callaíta" (May 2019) circulated during the perreo combativo protests. "Yo Perreo Sola" (2020) makes the feminist claim explicit. Together they trace the trajectory of the female dancing body from background to foreground. Edge label: perreo as resistance.

Safaera ↔ Baile Inolvidable

Formal relationship: both use music-historical reference as the primary political gesture. "Safaera" compresses reggaetón history; "Baile Inolvidable" compresses salsa/diaspora history. Both say: we remember where this came from.

Debí Tirar Más Fotos ↔ El Mundo Es Mío

Temporal arc: the earliest track (2016) and the title track (2025) frame the full nine-year political project. The assertion before the world agrees; the retrospective grief at not having documented the world you were insisting was yours. Edge label: the archive begins here.

04
Political Timeline
Puerto Rican political events 2016–2026 in parallel with Bad Bunny's discography
Two parallel tracks: Political Events on the left and Bad Bunny Releases on the right. Every node is annotated. The timeline's argument is in the proximity of events to releases — the music is always responding to something real.
Political Events
Bad Bunny Releases
2016
Political Event
PROMESA Signed (June 30, 2016)

Congress created La Junta — an unelected fiscal oversight board with broad authority over Puerto Rico's budget. Critics called it an extension of colonial control, removing democratic authority from elected officials. Puerto Rico carried $72 billion in public debt.

Bad Bunny Release
"El Mundo Es Mío" — SoundCloud single

First viral track, self-uploaded while working as a supermarket bagger. "The World Is Mine" — the opening declaration of a decade-long political project: the assertion of Puerto Rican selfhood against a system that denied it. Released the same year democratic authority was most explicitly overridden.

2017
Political Event
Hurricane Maria (September 20, 2017)

Category 4 storm devastates Puerto Rico: 2,975 deaths (suppressed to 64 for nearly a year), $91.6 billion in damage, entire power grid destroyed, 11 months to full restoration. 135,000+ Puerto Ricans emigrate to the mainland. Trump visits briefly, throws paper towels at a crowd.

Bad Bunny Release
No major release — Somos Live! benefit concert

Participated in hurricane relief telethon wearing a shirt reading "¿Eres Twitero o Presidente?" criticizing Trump's Twitter activity during the emergency. The absence of a full release is itself significant on the timeline: the island was not in a position to celebrate.

2018
Political Event
Maria Death Toll Cover-Up Exposed

Harvard NEJM study (August 2018) estimates 4,645 deaths. September 2018 government-commissioned study confirms 2,975. Governor Rosselló accepts new figure after nearly a year of denial. The cover-up of mass death became the central grievance fueling the 2019 protests.

Bad Bunny Release
X 100PRE — "Estamos Bien" (December 24, 2018)

Debut album released Christmas Eve as the death toll cover-up was being publicly reckoned with. Tonight Show performance opened: "After one year of the hurricane, there's still people without electricity — more than 3,000 people died, and Trump is still in denial." Bad Bunny's political voice breaks into American consciousness for the first time.

2019
Political Event
Kevin Fret Murdered (January 10, 2019)

Puerto Rico's first openly gay trap artist shot and killed in Santurce. Bad Bunny and Residente stood outside La Fortaleza the same day demanding accountability for violence. The murder catalyzed the connection between LGBTQ+ rights and the broader civic movement.

Bad Bunny Release
"Callaíta" — Single (May 2019)

Released two months before the protests. Became part of the perreo combativo soundtrack when women used the dance as protest outside La Fortaleza in July. The political meaning accrued retroactively through the protests.

Political Event
"RickyLeaks" / Telegramgate & Rosselló Resignation (July 13–24)

July 13: CPI publishes ~900 pages of chats — jokes mocking Maria dead, homophobic slurs, corruption references. Ten days of protests; ~500,000 people march July 22 — largest in Puerto Rican history. July 24: Rosselló announces resignation. Effective August 2.

Bad Bunny Release
"Afilando los Cuchillos" ft. Residente & iLe (July 17, 2019)

Released four days after RickyLeaks broke. Bad Bunny flew back from his European tour. Circulated via WhatsApp across the island within hours. Became the official protest anthem. Rosselló resigned seven days later. The most direct relationship between a release and a political event in the full timeline.

2020
Political Event
Earthquake Sequence & Alexa Luciano Ruiz Murdered

6.4 magnitude earthquake near Guánica collapses power grid for the third time in three years (Jan 7). Feb 24: Trans woman Alexa Neulisa Luciano Ruiz shot in Toa Baja; police and media repeatedly misgender her. Bad Bunny's Tonight Show appearance March 4 features shirt: "MATARON A ALEXA, NO A UN HOMBRE CON FALDA."

Bad Bunny Release
YHLQMDLG — "Yo Perreo Sola" (February 29, 2020)

Released Leap Day, two days before the Tonight Show / Alexa shirt moment. Becomes Spotify's most-streamed album globally in 2020. "Yo Perreo Sola" and its drag video argue for every woman's right to occupy public space on her own terms — in direct conversation with Puerto Rico's femicide crisis.

2021
Political Event
LUMA Energy Takes Over Power Grid (June 1, 2021)

LUMA Energy — private Canadian-American consortium — begins operating Puerto Rico's transmission and distribution system. 15-year contract, $2.4 billion. Blackouts increase, rates rise. January 2021: Puerto Rico's governor declares state of emergency over femicide, connected to advocacy by Bad Bunny and iLe.

2022
Political Event
Ex-Governor Vázquez Arrested & Hurricane Fiona (Sept 2022)

Former Governor Wanda Vázquez arrested on federal bribery charges — first former Puerto Rican governor to face federal criminal charges. Hurricane Fiona: record rainfall, complete island-wide blackout, $3.2 billion in damage. LUMA's failure to maintain the grid widely blamed.

Bad Bunny Release
Un Verano Sin Ti — "El Apagón" (May 6, 2022)

Most streamed album globally in 2022 (IFPI). "El Apagón" directly targets LUMA and Governor Pierluisi; the 22-min. documentary Aquí Vive Gente addresses gentrification and Act 60. First Spanish-language album nominated for Grammy Album of the Year. Bad Bunny calls out LUMA from the stage at Choliseo in July 2022.

2024–25
Political Event
2024 Puerto Rico Gubernatorial Election

Bad Bunny publicly endorses Juan Dalmau of the Puerto Rican Independence Party — the first time in decades an independence candidate received such prominent celebrity support. Dalmau's platform: independence, anti-corruption, opposition to Act 60 and La Junta.

Bad Bunny Release
Debí Tirar Más Fotos (January 5, 2025)

Sixth studio album released two weeks before Trump's second inauguration. Blends plena, salsa, bomba, jíbaro, dembow. YouTube visualizers (w/ historian Jorell Meléndez-Badillo) provide full Puerto Rican history lesson. Wins Grammy for Album of the Year (68th Grammys) — first Spanish-language album in history to win. Bad Bunny dedicates the award to Puerto Rico.

Political Event
Super Bowl LX Halftime Show (February 8, 2026)

Bad Bunny performs at Super Bowl LX in Santa Clara, California (Levi's Stadium). Had initially removed all U.S. tour dates to protect fans from ICE targeting; accepted Super Bowl invitation explicitly as protest. "El Apagón" performed in full — lyrics about LUMA Energy and displacement broadcast to 100+ million viewers.

Political Event
ICE Raids in Puerto Rico (June 2025)

Bad Bunny posts video criticizing ICE deportation operations in Puerto Rico — a U.S. territory whose residents are American citizens. The contradiction: U.S. immigration enforcement targeting constitutionally American citizens exposes the colonial logic that treats Puerto Ricans as perpetual foreigners in their own homeland.
